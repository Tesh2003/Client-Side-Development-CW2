Why Coral Reefs Matter

What makes a coral reef?

Coral reefs are constructed by minute living creatures known as corals which are sea animals located in oceans worldwide. Not all corals are individual organisms, rather they are a colony consisting of lots of tiny identical structures called polyps. These polyps collaborate in the form of a single structure, the coral. With time, corals form hard skeletons that are calcium carbonate-based. With the growth and death of generations of corals their skeletons build up and form the solid base of a coral reef. Coral reefs are underwater structures that are large and complex, created by a large number of coral colonies. Coral reefs are the most diverse ecosystems on the planet. They are also the habitat of a large number of marine animals such as colourfully coloured fish, crustaceans, mollusks and other marine animals. The success of the coral reefs is due to the special association of the coral and microscopic algae residing within the corals. These algae perform photosynthesis, bringing energy that helps to grow corals and, consequently, sustains whole ecosystems.

The major characteristics of the Coral Reefs include:

1. One coral comprises of numerous small polyps that collaborate.

2. By laying hard skeletons, corals bit by bit construct reefs.

3. Coral reefs offer food, shelter and breeding areas to the marine animals.

Coral reefs are so important in sustaining the health of the marine environment. They sustain an amazing biodiversity with thousands of species living in their habitats. Moreover, reefs play a protective role as natural barriers to ensure strong waves, storms, and erosion are prevented on the coasts.

Coral reefs are also involved in other important natural processes like carbon and nitrogen cycling, which helps in the regulation of the ocean ecosystem. They are also useful to humans besides the benefits to the environment. Reefs are relevant to the world economy because many communities rely on them as a source of food, tourism and income. But, coral reefs face serious threats. Ocean temperatures are increasing due to climate change, which is causing coral to bleach. These vulnerable ecosystems are also being destroyed by pollution, overfishing, and destructive fishing methods. The conservation of the coral reefs is necessary to protect them so that they can survive to the coming generations.

Types of coral reefs

Hard Corals (Stony Corals)

The hard corals or stony corals are the ones that form the physical structure of the coral reefs. These are invertebrates of the sea that have hard shells composed of calcium carbonate. They are the scleractinia order, and are dependent on symbiotic algae (remed also known as zooxanthellae) to obtain energy by photosynthesis. The hard corals are usually found in the warm, tropical and subtropical waters and they are the primary reef builders.


Soft corals

Soft corals do not have rigid skeletons as hard corals. Rather, their bodies are flexible and held up by minute internal structures known as sclerites. They are also common in tropical oceans, and the subclass of octocorallia. They also feed on plankton by capturing it in the water like hard corals, but may also have symbiotic algae. Soft corals such as sea fans and leather corals are also examples, and they contribute to the complexity of habitat and diversity of reef ecosystems.
